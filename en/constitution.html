Constitution of Waikiki

Foundations

  1. The name of our country is Waikiki.
  2. Waikiki is an independent, sovereign nation with its own territory, government and citizenship.
  3. The form of government of Waikiki is a democratic dictatorship, led by a Dictator appointed for an unlimited term by Parliament.
  4. The country was established through the union of three provinces named Waikiki, Amazonia and Brazilia.
  5. The provinces of Waikiki are equal; movement, relocation or transport of goods between provinces may not be restricted in any case and no customs duties may be levied.
  6. The capital of Waikiki is Nova Aurelia, which serves as the administrative centre of the country and the seat of Parliament.
  7. The official language of Waikiki is English. The foundation of the country's modern culture is rooted in Anglo-Saxon culture.
  8. The official currency of Waikiki is the Waikiki Dollar, the issuance of which is the exclusive right of the National Bank.
  9. The primary duty of the state is to ensure prosperity, order, security and justice for its citizens.
  10. In the interest of maintaining peace and security, Waikiki participates in international institutions and agreements.
  11. No international organisation may restrict Waikiki's inalienable right of disposal over its territorial integrity, population, form of government and state structure.
  12. Waikiki is a market economy. The state may intervene in market processes only in the interest of the citizens' welfare.
  13. Waikiki enforces the principle of balanced, transparent and sustainable fiscal management, aimed at the accumulation of national wealth.
  14. The state may levy taxes to cover its expenses. When determining taxes, the principles of justice and equity must be taken into account.
  15. Our country defends its territorial and political sovereignty against external powers and terrorist organisations.
  16. Waikiki maintains a voluntary national defence reserve system. In a state of war, every citizen is obliged to perform national defence duties.
  17. Waikiki expects responsible conduct towards the national community from all its citizens.
  18. A priority goal of the state is to encourage civic self-reliance and individual initiative, with special regard to enterprise and innovation.

State Symbols

  1. The flag of Waikiki depicts two elephants, palm trees, a golden laurel wreath and three pyramids.
  2. The elephants symbolise peace, the palm trees harmony, the laurel wreath prosperity and the pyramids the alliance of the three provinces.
  3. The two halves of the country's coat of arms depict the harmony of traditional and modern values. The pyramids appearing in the upper part of the coat of arms show respect for ancient monuments.
  4. The symbol of Waikiki is a blue inscription containing the country's name embraced by a golden laurel wreath. Gold, white, turquoise and blue are the official colours of our country and the symbols of the royal family.
  5. The anthem of Waikiki is the song titled "This is our world".
  6. The King symbolises the unity of the nation; his person is sacred and inviolable.
  7. The royal title is inherited on the male line; the heir to the throne bears the title of Crown Prince. If there is no male descendant, the right passes to the King's eldest daughter.
  8. Members of the royal family do not qualify as public figures and cannot be held politically accountable.
  9. The King has the power to grant pardon to those convicted of committed offences.
  10. National and state holidays serve the preservation of traditions and qualify as public holidays.
  11. The national holidays of our country are:
    • Foundation Day, March 10.
    • Kingdom Day, August 4.
    • Peace Day, November 24.
  12. The state holidays of our country are:
    • Christmas, December 24 - 27.
    • New Year, December 31 - January 2.
    • Easter, the Monday following the first full moon after the spring equinox.
    • All Souls' Day, November 1.

National Values

  1. We respect the national unity created by the inhabitants of the country and the culture of the people of Waikiki and we protect it.
  2. Waikiki respects traditional values, the harmony of the nation and the family and as part of this supports childbearing.
  3. Our country is proud of the heritage of the Roman Empire and protects historical monuments and symbols.
  4. Waikiki protects natural assets, with special regard to rainforests, natural fresh waters and the surrounding seas.
  5. Waikiki pursues a protectionist economic policy aimed at creating national companies generating high added value.
  6. The country's natural resources, including fossil energy carriers, qualify as resources of priority national strategic importance and may not be transferred to foreign ownership.
  7. Family and marriage, as a community of life established between a man and a woman based on voluntary decision, stand under the special protection of the state.
  8. The care and upbringing of children is the natural right and primary obligation of parents. The state community watches over their activity.
  9. Based on its responsibility towards future generations, the state protects natural living conditions and animals within the framework of the constitutional order.
  10. We, the citizens of Waikiki, believe that the order of our country must be based on the cooperation of the nation.
  11. In the interest of preserving peace and security, as well as the sustainable development of humanity, Waikiki strives for cooperation with all peoples and countries of the world.
  12. We believe that the basis of prosperity is efficiency, therefore all state measures restricting this must be kept at a minimum level.
  13. We respect the achievements of our constitution and our national symbols, which embody the continuity of Waikiki's constitutional statehood and the unity of the nation.
  14. We believe that every adult who has reached the age of 18 is capable of making decisions of their own free will, taking individual responsibility and acting according to their desires.
  15. Waikiki ensures the intellectual and scientific enrichment of the nation through the support of education, research and innovation.
  16. The fundamental principle of our country's foreign policy is peaceful cooperation, mutual recognition of sovereignty and the deepening of economic partnership with other nations.
  17. The goal of the state is to ensure the opportunity for every citizen to unfold their abilities, for social advancement and for worthy living conditions.
  18. Waikiki expects from every citizen respect for the Constitution, compliance with the laws of the country and the promotion of the common good.
  19. We do not recognise the communist laws prior to 1999, because they formed the basis of a tyrannical rule, therefore we declare their invalidity and unlawfulness.
  20. The country's digital infrastructure qualifies as an asset of national strategic importance, the security and independence of which is guaranteed by the state.

Fundamental Rights

  1. Waikiki respects and protects human rights, as part of which every human being is free and legally equal.
  2. Every human being has the right to life and human dignity; the life of the foetus is entitled to protection from conception.
  3. Everyone has the right to freedom of thought, conscience and religion. This right includes the freedom to choose other beliefs.
  4. The state guarantees the right to private property, freedom of enterprise, freedom of speech and freedom of the press.
  5. The salary earned by citizens through honest work may not be burdened by any tax or contribution.
  6. Everyone has the right to freely develop their own personality, as long as this does not violate the rights of others and does not conflict with the constitutional order or moral law.
  7. Waikiki facilitates the provision of livelihood in old age by maintaining a unified state pension system and enabling the operation of voluntarily established social institutions.
  8. The law only has the right to prohibit acts harmful to society. What the law does not prohibit, no one may prevent, and what the law does not order, no one may be compelled to do.
  9. Everyone has the right to peaceful assembly and freedom of expression, provided that this does not violate the rights of others.
  10. Every person is innocent until proven guilty and everyone has the right to an independent judiciary.
  11. In all criminal proceedings, the accused has the right to a speedy and public trial held by an impartial jury.
  12. Citizens have the right to be protected in their persons, houses, papers and effects against unreasonable searches and seizures.
  13. Citizens have the right to know the legislation, government decisions affecting them and budget data. This information must be published in an easily understandable, searchable form and preserved indefinitely.
  14. Access to public interest information may only be restricted in exceptional cases, in the event of danger to national security or national strategy, based on a judicial decision.
  15. A fundamental right may be restricted to the necessary extent in order to enforce another fundamental right or to protect a constitutional value.

State Structure

  1. The source of public power is the people. The people exercise their power through their elected representatives or directly, within the framework of referendums.
  2. In the interest of efficiency, the state performs its tasks in a centralised manner, but also allows room for independent decision-making by local communities.
  3. The operation of the state is based on the principle of the separation of powers. Parliament exercises legislative power, the Government executive power, and the Supreme Court and lower courts judicial power.
  4. Legislation is jointly exercised by the two houses of Parliament, the Congress and the Senate. The Senate and Congress have an equal share of 100-100 votes.
  5. The Senate's function of advisory, control and preparation of long-term strategic decisions is intended to strengthen the balance of the state organisation.
  6. The Government is the supreme organ of public administration, which is responsible to the Congress, and may establish further public administration bodies as defined by law.
  7. The operation of the Government and the responsibility of its members are ensured by the control powers of the Congress, including the accountability and hearing of ministers.
  8. The administration of justice falls within the competence of the courts and the prosecution service, which serve the observance of laws and the settlement of disputes.
  9. The Supreme Court is obliged to give priority to the provisions of the Constitution over all other legislation and to create principles of uniformity of law in order to harmonise judicial practice.
  10. The National Bank, the Army, the Intelligence Service and the space agency of Waikiki constitute independent public administration and budgetary bodies. Further budgetary bodies may be established by priority law.
  11. The operation of the Intelligence Service may only take place within a legal framework, under parliamentary control, and may not violate constitutional fundamental rights and the principle of political neutrality.
  12. The task of the Army is to guarantee the external and internal security of the country, as well as to protect the population during natural disasters and states of emergency.

Parliament

  1. The two chambers of Parliament, the Congress and the Senate, may create priority laws within the framework of a joint plenary session, with a simple majority of the votes of those present.
  2. A plenary session may be convened by the Dictator, the President or the majority of the members of either chamber, which must be announced at least one week before the session.
  3. The plenary session has a quorum if at least two-thirds of the members of both the Congress and the Senate are present. Quorum must be recorded in the minutes at the beginning of the session.
  4. Either chamber of Parliament or its plenary session may authorise the establishment of committees of inquiry, which are entitled to summon witnesses, request documents and report on their findings.
  5. Members of Parliament may address interpellations and questions to the Government, and its members are obliged to give a substantive answer to the question.
  6. Sessions of Parliament are public, minutes and recordings available to the public are made of them, and the votes of representatives are also public.
  7. Detailed rules of the operation of Parliament and the order of legislation are laid down in the Standing Orders, which are adopted and may be amended by both chambers separately with a two-thirds majority.
  8. Parliament makes its decisions by majority vote, unless the Constitution prescribes a qualified majority.
  9. Parliament has the power to raise and support armies and to declare war with two-thirds of the votes.
  10. In accordance with the principle of popular sovereignty, Members of Congress are freely elected by citizens at regular intervals.
  11. The 100 seats in Congress are distributed among the candidates of the parties in proportion to the votes obtained in the elections.
  12. Every citizen who has the right to vote is eligible to be a member of Congress, unless convicted of an act that makes them unworthy in the eyes of the community.
  13. With the support of the majority of representatives, Congress may create legislation at the level of law. If this conflicts with a law passed by the Senate, a plenary session of Parliament is required.
  14. The majority of Members of Congress decide on the country's budget, taking into account priority laws on budgetary rules.
  15. During their mandate, representatives are obliged to follow the law and justice, are subordinate to the Constitution, and are not bound by any other instruction.
  16. Representatives may form standing committees and parliamentary groups to coordinate their activities, and Congress may determine its own agenda.
  17. During their mandate, representatives may not hold other central government positions, may not be ministers, judges, members of the army or the Monetary Council.
  18. The Dictator may freely decide on the composition and members of the Senate, and may dismiss members of the Senate or appoint new members at any time.
  19. The Senate decides on the budget of priority investment projects and monitors their implementation.
  20. The members of the Senate may create laws with a majority of their votes. If this conflicts with a law passed by Congress, Parliament discusses the issue within the framework of a plenary session.

Government

  1. Executive power is exercised by the Government elected by Congress, led by the President.
  2. The main task of the Government is to ensure the implementation of laws, implement the budget and protect the Constitution.
  3. The Government is responsible for the unified and efficient functioning of state administration, the maintenance of public order and the daily security of the population.
  4. The appointment of the Government requires at least the simple majority of the votes of the representatives.
  5. The appointment of the Government is for five years, and lasts from the first Parliamentary session of the year following the election, at the earliest from January 3.
  6. Before the expiry of its mandate, the Government may be dismissed within the framework of a motion of no confidence with the votes of at least two-thirds of the representatives.
  7. The structure of the government of Waikiki and the list of its ministers are laid down by law, the amendment of which requires a simple majority of the representatives.
  8. The list of ministers is approved by Congress on the proposal of the President, with a simple majority of the representatives present.
  9. The President determines the general policy of the Government, within the framework of which ministers independently manage the branches of public administration falling within their competence.
  10. The President represents the Government before Parliament and directs the coordination of government decisions with the Dictator and the Senate.
  11. The President may adjourn a vote on a bill in Congress a maximum of two times and for a maximum of seven days.
  12. Ministers may submit bills to Parliament and may issue decrees within their own field of expertise, as well as initiate a national referendum.
  13. The Minister of War appoints the five brigadier generals of the army, promotes the officers of the military and, together with the Minister of Defence, is responsible for the maintenance of the army and the defence of the country.
  14. The Minister of Culture appoints the President of the Media Council every seven years.
  15. The Minister of the Interior appoints the Director of the Intelligence Service every seven years.
  16. The Minister of Foreign Affairs appoints and recalls ambassadors and conducts diplomatic discussions with leaders of other nations.
  17. The Minister of Finance and the Minister of Trade participate in the meetings of both the Budget Council and the Monetary Council.
  18. The Minister of Justice participates in the preparation and commenting on bills, and proposes the person of the Chief Prosecutor.
  19. The Minister of Education directs the system of school curricula, higher education standards and scientific tenders, and is responsible for digital knowledge development.

Courts

  1. Waikiki has a four-level court system, which, in order of appeal, are the county courts, the regional courts, the provincial courts and the Supreme Court.
  2. The members of the Supreme Court and the Chief Justice of Waikiki are appointed by the Senate. Their mandate is for 7 years and may be extended an unlimited number of times.
  3. Members of the Supreme Court supervise the lawful operation of the government and compliance with the Constitution.
  4. The Supreme Court invalidates legislation in case of conflict with a higher law or the Constitution, and sends it back to the body that created it.
  5. The Supreme Court decides on unconstitutionality out of turn, but within thirty days at the latest; if it establishes unconstitutionality, it immediately repeals the legislation in question.
  6. The Supreme Court has exclusive jurisdiction to decide on disputes of competence between the Government and Parliament, as well as on questions of incompatibility of constitutional bodies.
  7. The judiciary of Waikiki is independent of the government. The Government may not restrict judicial independence in any way and may not exert pressure on judges.
  8. Judges may not be removed by the Government and their remuneration may not be reduced during their tenure.
  9. In their activities, judges are subordinate only to the law. Judges may only be dismissed in case of a criminal offence, by a final court judgment.
  10. Courts are obliged to hear their cases and make their decisions along the principles of transparency, timeliness and legal certainty.
  11. The appointment, inauguration, transfer and promotion of judges, as well as disciplinary accountability, is the right of the Supreme Council of the Judiciary in accordance with the law, the members of which are elected by judges from among themselves.
  12. Ethical rules for judges are laid down in a separate law, and their observance is supervised by the Supreme Council of the Judiciary.
  13. The prerequisite for judicial appointment is a law degree, professional experience, impeccable moral character and a successful aptitude test.
  14. A priority law determines the further requirements and rules based on which the judges of the courts are appointed.
  15. Practising judges may not hold other public functions, except for unpaid educational activities or legal scientific research, as defined by law.
  16. Military courts have jurisdiction defined by law in wartime. In peacetime, their jurisdiction extends only to military offences committed by members of the armed forces.
  17. In the application of law, courts interpret the text of legislation primarily in accordance with their purpose and the Constitution, assuming that they serve a moral and economic purpose consistent with common sense and the public good.
  18. The law may offer alternative proposals for the settlement of disputes which are implemented without the involvement of courts, and arbitration chosen with the consent of the parties may also be applied in cases between citizens.
  19. The state undertakes to ensure the financial, material and technical conditions necessary for the operation of courts, as well as an environment befitting the dignity of the administration of justice.

Local Government

  1. Local governments operate in Waikiki for the management of local public affairs and the exercise of local public power. The legal status of local governments is regulated by a priority law.
  2. Local governments may freely decide on the rules of coexistence and the budget of local communities, provided that this does not conflict with higher-ranking legislation.
  3. Local governments may create decrees within the framework of a local referendum, which are valid in the territory of the given local government.
  4. Local government decrees may not be contrary to legislation with national scope and may not infringe upon the legitimate interests of citizens.
  5. Local governments are led by the mayor, who is elected every five years within the framework of a local election.
  6. Any adult local resident may be elected mayor, but a Member of Congress may not be the mayor of a local government.
  7. The local community may freely decide on the structure of the local government body and the salaries of its members in the form of a referendum.
  8. Every citizen has the right to hold local public office in accordance with their aptitude, qualifications and professional knowledge.
  9. Every adult citizen residing locally may vote in local elections and local referendums, and every vote is of equal value.
  10. Local decrees and local governments may not apply discrimination based on gender, religion, wealth or ideology among their citizens.
  11. In case of legitimate grievance against local decrees, any citizen may turn to the court.
  12. Decisions on the expenses of local governments may be made within the framework of local referendums; cases deviating from this are laid down in a priority law.
  13. The result of a direct local referendum is binding on the local government, unless it is contrary to the Constitution or a priority law, which the Supreme Court is entitled to establish.
  14. Local governments receive a share of the central budget in proportion to the population, from which they finance their operation and investments.
  15. Local governments may not levy taxes and contributions, and may not take out loans in any form.
  16. The property of local governments is public property, which serves the performance of their tasks. Ownership rights over local government assets are exercised by the mayor.
  17. Local government bodies are entitled to establish temporary working groups and advisory bodies in matters of public interest, with the involvement of civil organisations and residents.
  18. Local communities hold a public forum at least once a year, where the mayor and the local government report on the use of public funds and decisions of public interest.

Legislation

  1. Waikiki is a democratic state governed by the rule of law. Compliance with the law is mandatory for everyone and its verification is the task of the state.
  2. The structure of the state organisation, fundamental human and civil rights, national symbols and the hierarchy of legislation are established by the Constitution.
  3. The amendment of the Constitution requires at least two-thirds of the total votes of Parliament and a majority of the votes of both chambers.
  4. Civil rights, international treaties and agreements, budgetary rules and the rights of the National Bank are contained in priority laws.
  5. The amendment of priority laws requires at least more than half of the combined votes of Senators and Congress representatives.
  6. Legislation adopted within the framework of a national referendum qualifies as a priority law.
  7. The adoption or amendment of laws requires a simple majority of the votes of the Senate or Congress.
  8. If a conflict arises between the Senate and Congress regarding a given piece of legislation, the enactment of a priority law is necessary.
  9. No legislation may be contrary to higher-ranking legislation in force. Verification of this falls within the competence of the Supreme Court.
  10. The hierarchical order of legislation: the Constitution, priority laws, laws and decrees. Resolutions regulating the internal operation of the state do not qualify as legislation.
  11. The Dictator, the President, the Chancellor and ministers within their own competence have the right to issue decrees with national scope.
  12. Local governments may create decrees with local scope. The adoption of local government decrees requires a local referendum.
  13. No greater authorisation may be required for the repeal of any legislation than is necessary for its adoption.
  14. A law may be initiated by the Dictator, the Chancellor, the President, members of the Government, Members of Congress or any member of the Senate.
  15. The adopted law is sent by Parliament to the Dictator within five days, who signs the sent law within five days and orders its promulgation, or exercises his veto right.
  16. If any representative considers a law or any of its provisions to be contrary to the Constitution, they may send it to the Supreme Court for examination.
  17. All submitted bills and amendments must be traceable and available to the public in an easily understandable form.
  18. Parliamentary appointments, resolutions and legislation for which a simple majority of votes is sufficient according to the Constitution may also be adopted in an expedited procedure, provided that at least two-thirds of the members of Parliament support the proposal.
  19. An expedited procedure may not be used for the amendment of the Constitution, the declaration of a state of war or state of danger, the conclusion of peace, the initiation of a referendum, or in cases where the Constitution prescribes a higher level or qualified majority requirement.

Special Legal Order

  1. If Waikiki is attacked within its state borders, the majority of Members of Congress, with the countersignature of the Dictator, are entitled to declare a state of war.
  2. In a state of war, constitutional rights and the operation of Parliament may be restricted to the extent strictly necessary for national defence, and elections may be postponed until the end of the special legal order.
  3. In a state of war, the right to private property may also be restricted, but upon its cessation, immediate and appropriate compensation must be paid to those concerned.
  4. In the event of natural disasters or other series of events endangering masses, a state of danger may be introduced with the votes of two-thirds of the representatives.
  5. If Congress faces an unavoidable obstacle in making decisions due to events triggering the state of war or state of danger, the Dictator may also declare it.
  6. The fact of the obstacle, as well as the justification for the declaration of the state of war or state of danger, is established unanimously by the Dictator, the President and the Chief Justice.
  7. Upon the cessation of the state of danger or state of war, the special legal order must be terminated immediately, and the special legal order may not be used for the unlawful exercise of power.
  8. In case of a state of danger, the government may amend the budget by decree to the necessary extent and may temporarily create legislation at the level of law.
  9. During a state of danger, the Government may issue a decree by which it may suspend the application of certain laws, deviate from statutory provisions, and take other extraordinary measures.
  10. Parliament may monitor the work of the Government even in case of a state of danger, and may subsequently approve or reject legislation adopted within the framework of decree governance.
  11. In case of a special legal order, the application of the Constitution may not be suspended and the operation of the Supreme Court may not be restricted.
  12. The special legal order is terminated by the organ entitled to introduce it if the conditions for its declaration no longer exist. In this case, the temporarily adopted legislation loses its validity.
  13. Regulations concerning the state of danger may also be applied in a state of war, with the proviso that a state of danger may also be declared in case of immediate danger of armed attack.
  14. For the conclusion of peace, two-thirds of Parliamentary votes or a national referendum is required. If the conditions for this do not exist, the Dictator may also conclude peace, which he submits to Parliament as soon as he has the opportunity.

Citizenship

  1. A child of Waikiki parents is a citizen from birth.
  2. Anyone born on the territory of Waikiki, provided at least one parent is a citizen, receives citizenship rights.
  3. Anyone may apply for citizenship rights; the order and requirements of the procedure are laid down in a priority law.
  4. Waikiki protects its citizens from both external and internal threats. In the interest of compliance with laws, the state may use force.
  5. Legally acquired citizenship may only be withdrawn in exceptional cases, in the event of the commission of crimes dangerous to national security, based on a final court judgment.
  6. No distinction may be applied between citizenship acquired by birth and by other means.
  7. With the exception of security or health reasons ordered by law, every citizen may move freely and reside in any part of the country's territory.
  8. Every citizen may freely leave the territory of Waikiki and freely return there, with the exception of cases defined by law.
  9. Every citizen has the right, upon request, to receive objective information about the activities of the state and public authorities.
  10. Every citizen has the right to work on the territory of Waikiki and to establish companies or acquire ownership shares in other companies.
  11. In the interest of livelihood in old age, citizens are entitled to a pension, the age limit and amount of which are established by a priority law.
  12. In the interest of supporting families, mothers giving birth to a child may receive an allowance for the duration of the care of their infant, the details of which are regulated by a priority law.
  13. The government of Waikiki ensures employment opportunities for all its citizens, thereby creating the resources necessary for self-reliance.
  14. Every citizen is entitled to participate in the state education system, where they may receive appropriate primary, secondary and university education.
  15. Citizens may use the state healthcare system, in which the Government provides services defined by priority law free of charge.
  16. Waitki grants further rights to citizens in priority laws, which may be amended within the framework of a plenary session of Parliament or by referendum.

Right to Vote

  1. Waikiki holds democratic elections every 5 years, in which every voting citizen who has reached the age of 18 may participate.
  2. Every adult citizen who is not barred from public affairs by a final court judgment may participate in both elections and referendums.
  3. Elections are direct, proportional and free; citizens' votes are secret and equal.
  4. Congressional elections are single-round; votes may be cast for the presidential candidate nominated in advance by the parties and the corresponding party lists.
  5. To participate as a party in the election, at least 1 million signatures of support must be collected, and to form a parliamentary group, at least 5% of the votes cast are required.
  6. The number of Congressional seats won by a party corresponds to the percentage of votes it achieved, taking into account fractional percentages in descending order.
  7. No party's activity may be aimed at the violent acquisition or exclusive possession of public power, nor may it be contrary to constitutional values.
  8. Every voter has the right to propose a referendum, support the proposals of others and participate in referendums.
  9. On the proposal of at least five hundred thousand voters, the President orders a national referendum, which is valid in all cases and binding on Parliament.
  10. A national referendum may also be initiated by the Dictator, the President, the Senate and Congress, in which case the support of five hundred thousand voters is not required.
  11. Legislation adopted within the framework of referendums qualifies as priority laws and may override other priority laws or previous referendum results.
  12. A national referendum may not be held on questions aimed at amending the Constitution, on the personal composition of the government, on obligations arising from international treaties, or on the declaration of a state of war.
  13. Referendum initiatives may be reviewed by the Supreme Court, and if it conflicts with a fundamental value, a general moral value or is identical in content to a referendum held within one year, it may refuse to call the referendum.
  14. Every citizen with a permanent address who may participate in national elections may also vote in municipal elections and local referendums according to their place of residence.
  15. Citizens of Waikiki may cast their votes even far from their place of residence, in online form, or in person at local governments.

Dictatorial Rights

  1. The Dictator exercises executive power in accordance with the Constitution and the laws of the country, shared with the Government.
  2. The Dictator may submit bills and other measures to Parliament and may submit referendum initiatives.
  3. The Dictator may exercise the right of veto against bills adopted by Parliament, in which case the law does not enter into force.
  4. The Dictator may issue a decree on the amount of money issued by the National Bank or on other issues not regulated by law.
  5. Dictatorial decrees are legislation lower than the law, but higher than other decrees.
  6. The Dictator decides on the structure, members and salaries of the Senate. The Dictator may freely modify the composition of the Senate.
  7. The Dictator appoints the Chancellor of Waikiki, who serves as the Dictator's deputy in his absence and is also the President of the Budget Council.
  8. Waitki appoints the members of the Monetary Council and the Governor of the National Bank directly. The appointment of the Governor of the National Bank is for seven years.
  9. The Dictator may appoint advisors and the expenses of his cabinet are covered by the state budget with the approval of Congress.
  10. The Dictator may also speak outside the agenda at sessions of Parliament and may submit interpellations to members of the Government.
  11. The Dictator appoints the Chief of the General Staff of the army, who is also the Commander-in-Chief of the armed forces and his mandate is for seven years.
  12. In a state of war, the Dictator may appoint a National Defence Council and may temporarily take over the command of the army.
  13. In case of a state of emergency, the Dictator may issue temporary legislation, provided that it is not contrary to the Constitution. These must be submitted immediately for confirmation when Parliament is convened.
  14. In the event of the Dictator's resignation, his successor is appointed by Parliament, for which at least two-thirds of the votes are required.
  15. If the Dictator is unable to perform his duties, the Chancellor takes over his office until Parliament elects a new Dictator. Further members of the line of succession are regulated by a priority law.

Public Funds

  1. Congress enacts a law on the central budget and its implementation for each year and votes on it by the end of the previous year at the latest.
  2. Congress may not adopt a law on the central budget resulting in the state's planned expenditures exceeding its revenues.
  3. The Government is obliged to implement the central budget lawfully and expediently, with effective management of public funds and ensuring transparency.
  4. The property of the state and local governments is national wealth. National wealth may only be transferred for purposes defined by law, taking into account the requirement of proportionality of value.
  5. With the exception of the property of independent budgetary bodies and local governments, the entire national wealth is one and indivisible.
  6. The most important public goods provided by the state are national defence, law enforcement, justice, education, healthcare, transport and environmental protection, which are covered by the central budget.
  7. The budget is prepared by the Budget Council, of which the permanent members are the Dictator, the Chancellor, the Minister of Finance and the Minister of Trade. Further members may be appointed by Congress.
  8. The budget for a given year may be amended by Congress with a two-thirds majority on the initiative of the President. Exceptions to this are unexpected expenditures and revenues deviating from the planned, in which case a simple majority of representatives is sufficient.
  9. If a budget surplus for a given year exceeds the planned amount, the accumulated amount is spent by the state on priority investments in the following year.
  10. The subsystems of the state, including ministries, offices and companies with unlimited liability on the part of the state, may not become indebted in any form.
  11. The primary task of the National Bank is to ensure price stability. This may not be overridden by any other economic policy objective.
  12. The National Bank manages the country's gold reserves and the investments of the national wealth fund. The investment principles of the National Bank are laid down in a priority law.
  13. The Governor of the National Bank reports annually on his work to Parliament and presents the inflation report, which must be approved by Parliament.
  14. The National Bank pays its profit into the budget of the given year without delay, and covers its possible loss from the budget of the given year.

Justice

  1. Everyone has the right to have any charge against them or their rights and obligations in a lawsuit judged by an independent and impartial court in a fair and public trial within a reasonable time.
  2. No one may be considered guilty until their criminal liability has been established by a final decision of a court.
  3. A person subject to criminal proceedings has the right to defence counsel at all stages of the proceedings.
  4. No one may be convicted for an act that was not expressly classified as a crime or offence by law at the time it was committed.
  5. With the exception of extraordinary cases of legal remedy, no one may be convicted for a crime for which they have already been finally acquitted or convicted in Waikiki.
  6. Human life is sacred and inviolable, therefore the death penalty may not be applied against citizens of Waikiki in any case.
  7. Imprisonment may only be imposed in case of crimes, in cases prescribed by law or higher-level legislation.
  8. In case of imprisonment, the convicted person is obliged to reimburse the full cost of their detention to the state.
  9. The Supreme Court has appellate jurisdiction on both questions of law and fact. The Supreme Court may modify the judgments of lower courts.
  10. All crimes, with the exception of Supreme Court cases, are tried by a jury.
  11. The Chief Prosecutor and the prosecution service are independent, and as a contributor to the administration of justice, as a public prosecutor, are the exclusive enforcer of the state's punitive claim.
  12. The prosecution service prosecutes crimes, takes action against other unlawful acts and omissions, and promotes the prevention of unlawful acts.
  13. The Chief Prosecutor is elected from among the prosecutors by Congress on the proposal of the Minister of Justice for seven years.
  14. The prosecution organisation is led and directed by the Chief Prosecutor, who appoints prosecutors and reports annually on his activities to Parliament.
  15. Prosecutors are responsible officials subordinate in rank, who may not be transferred, suspended, retired or dismissed, with the exception of cases defined by law.
